Central Florida's unique climate — high humidity, summer thunderstorms, aggressive pest pressure, and many homes 30+ years old — makes Orlando residences especially prone to electrical trouble. Here are the top 5 issues we diagnose across Orlando every month, why they happen here specifically, and what to do about each.

## 1. Frequently Tripping Circuit Breakers

A breaker's job is to trip when something's wrong — a short, an overload, or a ground fault. Occasional trips are normal. Monthly trips are not.

Common causes in Orlando homes:

- **Overloaded circuit:** too many appliances on one breaker (often a kitchen or laundry outlet).
- **Humid panel connections:** Florida humidity corrodes bus bars and breaker contacts over time.
- **Failed appliance:** a motor going bad (AC, pool pump, garbage disposal) can trip repeatedly.

**Fix:** identify which circuit is affected, unplug appliances on it, and test. If trips continue, call a professional — repeated tripping points to wiring issues that won't resolve themselves.

## 2. Flickering or Dimming Lights

Lights that flicker briefly when a heavy appliance turns on are usually a voltage-drop symptom — the panel can't deliver enough current fast enough.

**Common in Orlando:** homes built in the 1980s and 1990s with aluminum branch wiring can have loose or oxidized connections at outlets and switches. These are a real fire hazard if ignored.

**Fix:** a thorough inspection of panel connections, main lugs, and neutral bus. If aluminum wiring is present, pigtailing with copper or selective replacement is often the safest long-term path.

## 3. Outdated Wiring (Aluminum or Knob-and-Tube)

Many Orlando homes built between 1965 and 1975 have aluminum branch wiring. Older homes in Winter Park, College Park, and Delaney Park sometimes still have knob-and-tube hiding inside the walls.

Both are insurance concerns — many home insurers refuse coverage on knob-and-tube, and some require CO/ALR-rated devices everywhere with aluminum present.

**Fix:** partial rewiring with copper in high-use areas (kitchens, bathrooms, laundry), CO/ALR devices everywhere else, or full rewiring for older homes that are due for a deeper renovation.

## 4. Dead Outlets or Switches

A single outlet or switch that stops working usually means one of three things:

- **Tripped GFCI upstream:** many kitchen and bathroom outlets are protected by a single GFCI somewhere in the circuit. Reset it first.
- **Loose back-wire connection:** outlets wired by pushing wires into a back-stab port (common in older DIY homes) loosen over time.
- **Burned device:** especially on outlets that were plugged in for space heaters or other high-draw appliances.

**Fix:** if reset doesn't work, the outlet needs replacement — ideally with spec-grade devices, screw-terminal wiring, and GFCI protection if appropriate for the location.

## 5. No GFCI Protection in Wet Areas

Current code requires GFCI protection on outlets in kitchens, bathrooms, garages, outdoors, and near wet bars. Many Orlando homes from before 2005 don't have this protection in place.

GFCI trips in 1/40th of a second when it detects current leaking to ground — preventing serious shock and electrocution.

**Fix:** install GFCI outlets (about $30–$50 per location) or GFCI breakers at the panel for whole-circuit protection. Cheap insurance for wet-area safety.

## When to Call a Professional

Anything involving panel work, new circuit installation, or troubleshooting beyond replacing an outlet should be handled by a licensed electrician. Florida law and most home insurance policies require licensed, insured work.
